## 📝 Phrases for Salary Discussions

### Before the Offer
- "Could you share the salary range for this role?"
- "I am flexible based on the total compensation package."
- "I would like to understand the benefits before discussing salary."

### Receiving an Offer
- "Thank you for the offer. I am very excited about this opportunity."
- "I appreciate the offer. Could I take a day to review the details?"
- "I am very interested, but I would like to discuss the compensation."

### Negotiating
- "Based on my skills and experience, I was hoping for a salary closer to $55,000."
- "Is there room for negotiation on the base salary?"
- "I understand the budget. Could we discuss other benefits like training or flexible hours?"

### When to Accept
- "This sounds fair. I would be happy to accept."
- "Thank you. I look forward to starting."

## 📝 Full Negotiation Dialogue
**Interviewer:** "We would like to offer you the position. The starting salary is $48,000."
**You:** "Thank you so much. I am very excited about this role. Based on my experience in data analysis and my finance background, I was hoping for something in the range of $52,000 to $55,000. Is there flexibility on the salary?"
**Interviewer:** "I understand. Let me check with HR and get back to you."
**You:** "I appreciate that. I am very committed to this role and I am confident I can deliver great results for the team."

## 📖 Grammar Point: Polite Requests for Negotiation
| Phrase | Level of Politeness | Example |
|--------|---------------------|---------|
| "Can you..." | Neutral | "Can you increase the offer?" |
| "Could you..." | More polite | "Could you consider a higher salary?" |
| "Would it be possible to..." | Very polite | "Would it be possible to discuss relocation support?" |
| "I was wondering if..." | Very polite | "I was wondering if the salary is negotiable." |

## 💡 Interview Tip
**In Canada, salary negotiation is normal and expected.** Do not feel guilty about asking. However, always be professional and appreciative. Never threaten or give ultimatums. A good rule: ask for 10-20% more than the initial offer if you have relevant skills.
